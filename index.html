How to Make a Character

  • Roll your attributes
  • Pick a class
  • Choose equipment
  • Name your character
  • Roll height and age
  • Decide on other personal characteristics (personality, eye color, etc.)

You can choose to do these in any order you like

Attributes

Attributes are rated from 0-10. To determine your starting attribute, roll 2d6-2 for each one.

Attributes are mostly meant to determine what your character is like and for adjudicating certain situations (roll dexterity to pick locks for example).

Attribute Description
Strength Examples: Smash door, climb tree
Agility Examples: Balance, sneak
Dexterity Examples: Picking a lock or pocket
Intelligence Examples: Decipher script, identify magic item
Charisma Examples: Diplomacy, Bluffing
Wisdom Examples: Binding a wound
Luck Examples: Secret
Comeliness Examples: How pretty you are

Attributes do not change your attacks, saving throws, AC, etc.

Leveling Up

A character who has earned sufficient XP may level up by spending some amount of downtime and resources to train.

A character can only level up during downtime periods.

Gaining multiple levels is permitted.

How to Level Up

Leveling up is mostly self-explanatory: Update your values according to your class's table and make any choices offered to you.

Hit points are rolled using the Sticky-Up method.

Sticky-Up HP Rolling

When you gain a new level, roll the dice indicated in your class's HP column. If you rolled equal to or lower than your previous HP total, your HP increases by 1. Otherwise, your HP becomes the number you rolled.

Thus, your HP is rerolled at each level, but can only go up (sticky-up).

Adventuring Rules

Jumping

To determine how far you can jump horizontally, roll 2d10 + your strength.

To determine how far you can jump vertically, roll d10 + your strength.

In practice, the target number is typically set by what you are trying to jump across or to and you need to roll that number.

You can jump a horizontal distance up to your speed and a vertical distance up to triple your height, rounded up to the nearest 5' increment.

For every size category smaller than medium, you get -4 to your jump checks. For every size category larger than medium, add +4 to your jump checks.

For every 5' slower than 30', you get -2 to jump. For every 5' faster than 30', you get +2 to jump.

Jumping in combat is normally a part of your movement for the turn.

If something allows to jump as a specific action, then the amount of distance you jump is extra movement granted by that action.

In combat, you cannot jump farther than your remaining movement for the round.

Double Jeopardy

In almost all cases, the same action cannot be attempted twice. For example, if a character fails to pick a lock, the party cannot retry that lock. They will need to do something substantively different, usually escalating the situation.

Natural Healing

Characters heal their level in hit points each night. If they rest all day, they heal twice as much.

Food and Water

After 3 days, a character must make a fortitude save (DC 10 + 1 for each day without food beyond the 3 days) each day or suffer 1d6 unhealable damage.

Each day of food heals 1d6 of this damage.

After 1 day without water, a character must make a fortitude save (DC 10 + 5 for each day without water) each day or suffer 3d6 unhealable damage.

Each day of water heals 1d6 of this damage.

If characters go on half-rations, they suffer -2 to all d20 rolls but eat half as many rations each day and stave off the starvation damage.

Heat and Humidity

A character in excessive clothing or wearing armor suffers 1d6 unhealable heat exhaustion damage each hour in hot and humid climates.

An hour in cool shade with water heals 1d6 of this damage.

Cold and Freezing

A characer in inadequate clothing suffers 1d6 unhealable freezing damage each hour in cold or arctic climates.

An hour in shelter with fire heals 1d6 of this damage.

In even extremer cold, the rate of damage is increased.

Suffocation

A character without air suffers 1d6 suffocation damage each minute without air, beginning at 2 minutes.

Crusader Maneuvers

Due to your weak tie to Venrik, you occasionally are seized with divine inspiration. When not stressed due to battle or other extenuating circumstances, choose 5 of the maneuvers you know. Those maneuvers are prepared for you.

Management of maneuvers is simulated by use of playing cards. Each card represents one of the maneuvers you have prepared. At the beginning of your first turn of the encounter draw three (3) cards at random from the 5 maneuvers you've prepared. At the beginning of your following turns, draw an additional card from the maneuvers you haven't drawn yet. When you use a maneuver, place the card in your discard pile. If you have no more maneuvers you can draw, discard your current hand of cards and shuffle it into your discard pile. That pile then becomes the new pool you can draw from. Immediately draw two cards.

At higher levels you will know more maneuvers than you can prepare. To change your prepared maneuvers, you can spend 10 minutes meditating and praying to Venrik.

When you gain a level in this class, you can choose a maneuver you know and replace it with a different maneuver of the same level.

There are 3 kinds of maneuvers: Strikes, Counters and Boosts.

Unless otherwise specified, strikes are a standard action to use and involve you making a melee standard attack. Counters are always a reaction action. Unless otherwise specified, boosts are swift actions.

Enlightened Focus

As a disiciple of the enlightened, you have gained the ability to bring order to the chaotic elements of your soul. By meditating for 10 minutes, you can remove the chaos from your system.

This state of balance lasts until it is disrupted. Roll a d6 at the beginning of each turn in a stressful situation like combat. If you roll a 4, 5, or 6, your balance is disrupted. By disrupting it, you achieve a temporary state of enlightenment, walking in the footsteps of those that came before you Gentle Breeze and Kruglauk.

While in this state of enlightenment, you gain the following benefits:

  • You partially ignore the tragedy of flesh You take half damage from all sources.
  • Your mind is shielded from the influence of others You automatically succeed on any saving throws against fear, rage, or other mental effects such as mind control.
  • Your blows strike swift and true You can make an unarmed melee standard attack as a free action each round.

These benefits last until your are unconscious, the battle is over or 10 minutes pass, whichever comes soonest.

Unarmed and Unarmored

While wearing no armor, your base AC is higher than 10. The value is given in your class table. You are deftly able to dodge blows due to your ability to perceive reality in a different way.

Additionally, while wearing no armor your speed is increased by 10.

You are deadly with the fist. Your unarmed strikes deal extra damage and can be dual-wielded.

You do not receive a penalty when attempting an unarmed disarm attempt.

You do not need to be unarmed to make unarmed attacks.

Disciple Discoveries

As each Disciple of the enlightened makes their way in the world, they discover new fragments of enlightenment. Choose a discovery from the list below.

Abundant Step
You have discovered that movement is an illusion, as all is one. This discovery is hard to wrap your mind around, but allows you to appear wherever you desire, as there is no "where". You can appear at any "location" you can see instantaneously as a free action up to "600 feet away". You cannot bring anything with you except the clothes on your back and what you hold in your hands. Once you have done this, you will need to meditate as even after you perform it you form pearls of doubt. You can't do this again until you complete your 10 minute meditation where you remove chaos from your system.
Deflection
You can deflect arrows as a reaction, taking no damage from the one missile. As a full-round action, you can deflect all missiles that would strike you until the beginning of your next turn.
Empty Body
You have discovered how to very briefly not be somewhere and then immediately reappear. It is so brief that it requires great reflexes to achive, but is very useful when trying to avoid a dragon's breath. When you make a reflex saving throw to take half damage, you take no damage on a succesful saving throw and half damage on a failed saving throw.
Laws of Momentum
You can use an enemy's momentum against them. Whenever an enemy attacks you but misses, you make make a standard melee attack against them as a reaction.
Leap of the Clouds
Your jump distance is no longer limited by your speed, height or size. In combat, you can jump once per round without it costing you any movement. You ignore any penalty for being small or slow and you can jump any vertical distance that you can roll.
Purity of Body
You have discovered that hunger and thirst are illusions. You no longer need to eat or drink and cannot be diseased or poisoned.
Thickness of Air
You have discovered that air is as thick as we choose to perceive it to be. You can use this fact to slow and cushion your movements. You take no damage from being thrown or falling.
Tongue of the Sun and Moon
You have discovered the proto-language of all humanoids. You can speak to any humanoids via it, although the concepts you can communicate are limited to basic ones.
Uncanny Awareness
You have discovered the presence of a vestigial, ethereal third eye that we all have. You are never surprised or flat-footed.

Combat

Initiative and the Flow of Combat

At the beginning of combat, each side rolls a d20. The side of the high roller gets to go first. Each turn after that alternates between the two sides.

All characters on a side can take their turns and actions at the same time on their side's turn, or process them one at a time.

The beginning or end of a turn for a character is synonymous with the beginning or end of a turn for that character's side.

If one side has achieved surprise over the other, they get a free round before initiative is rolled. During this round, the members of their side can only take a single standard action each. The side that has been surprised is considered flat-footed.

If the d20 roll is a tie, it is a simultaneous turn. First one of the players goes, then one of the monsters goes, then one of the players goes, etc. The players can choose who they want to go, but they must choose within 10 seconds or lose their chance to go.

Actions in Combat

During each round, you may take a move-action, standard-action, swift-action and reaction.

A standard-action and a move-action may be combined together to take a full-action.

A standard-action can be used to take a move-action.

A standard-action can be readied to be performed contingent on some other visible action or effect. Readying an action this way does not require your reaction. The trigger must be perceivable by the character from an in-game perspective. For example, you can ready an action for when someone attacks any ally or draws their weapon, but not contingent on the start of someone's turn.

Swift-actions must happen during your turn. Any action that requires a swift-action will specify when it may be used. No other action type may be substituted for a swift-action.

Reactions can be taken during your turn or during anothers turn. Once you have taken a reaction you cannot take one until the beginning of your next turn.

A free action does not require the use of any action (it is free). However, some free actions may be limited in how many times they can be repeated.

Calculating Attack Bonus

Normally, your attack bonus is your Base Attack Bonus (BAB) from your class, plus any circumstantial modifiers. Some items, class features or other effects might also grant you a bonus to your attacks.

Critical Hits

If you roll a natural 20 (or another natural number in your weapon's critical hit range), you have threatened a critical hit. Immediately make another attack roll with the same bonus. If that attack roll hits, then you have confirmed the critical hit. By default, weapons have a x2 critical hit multiplier. This means you roll your damage and add your bonuses twice. For example, if you deal 1d6+3 damage on a normal hit, a critical hit will deal 1d6+3 + 1d6+3 = 2d6+6 damage.

Some weapons have x3 or even x4 critical hit multipliers. In those cases, roll your damage and add your bonuses that many times instead.

Spells with attack rolls can critically hit. They threaten on a natural 20 and have a x2 multiplier. If the spell does not deal damage, then critical hits have no extra effect.

Some weapons specify a special effect upon a critical hit. Look to those weapons for the description.

Attack of Opportunity

An attack of opportunity is a reaction that is performed in response to some activity that lowers the opponent's guard.

Some actions specify they provoke an attack of opportunity.

Additionally, a character moving away from you or moving within your reach provokes an attack of opportunity from you.

If a character has reach and you attempt to move beyond the edge of their reach towards them, you also provoke an attack of opportunity, even if you are moving into their deadzone.

Ranged Attacks provoke an attack of opportunity.

Most spells provoke attacks of opportunity. Melee attack spells, touch spells cast on allies, or spells originating from the caster with a length or radius of 15 ft or less do not provoke attacks of opportunity.

Drinking a potion or getting something out of your pack provokes an attack of opportunity.

Standing up from prone provokes an attack of opportunity.

If a character provokes an attack of opportunity from you, you may make an immediate, melee standard attack against them. You must be wielding a melee weapon in order to make this attack. If your unarmed strikes deal lethal damage, you are considered to be wielding a melee weapon for this purpose.

If you can somehow make more than one attack of opportunity each round, you can only make one for each opportunity. In this case, movement through your threatened spaces is only considered a single opportunity.

If a creature provokes an attack of opportunity from more than one creature, only two creatures may actually make an attack of opportunity.

Grapple
Standard Action

To start a grapple, you and the opponent make opposed grapple check.

A grapple check is rolled by making an unarmed attack roll.

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger and creatures smaller than medium get a -4 penalty for each category smaller.

If you win this check, you and your opponent are now grappling (both grappled) and you deal damage as if you hit with an unarmed strike.

You can't force a creature two sizes larger than you to grapple and such a creature can automatically escape such a grapple if he finds himself in one.

You and your opponent are both considered to be occupying both your space and their space.

Any ranged attacks into the grapple are equally likely to hit the other person instead on accident.

If the creatures are of different size, the ratio is adjusted accordingly.

Multiple creatures can be in a grapple.

Once a grapple is initiated, it is an attack to join the grapple, but you do not need to beat any grapple check to join.

If you need to roll an opposed check while in the grapple, pick an enemy grappler at random to oppose you.

If you are grappled:

  • You don't threaten any spaces with attacks of opportunity.
  • You are considered flat-footed against attackers not in the grapple.
  • You can't move from the grapple (see below).
  • You can't attack with weapons larger than light weapons.
  • You can't attack outside of the grapple.
  • You can't cast most spells (exception: instant teleports)
  • You can't draw weapons (see below) or items.
  • You can't dual-wield.

Attacks while grappling:

  • Attack with a natural or light weapon at a -4 penalty.
  • Damage your opponent. Make an opposed grapple check. If you win, you deal unarmed strike damage. Draw a light-weapon. This is a standard action and requires winning an opposed grapple check.
  • Move the grapple. You can move the grapple up to half your speed by winning an opposed grapple check.
  • Escape the grapple. You must win an opposed grapple check (you may use Escape) against the enemy grappler in the grapple. He gets +2 to this check for every ally he has in the grapple.
  • Pin an opponent. You must win an opposed grapple check (he may use Escape) against the enemy grappler in the grapple. You get +2 to this check for every ally you have in the grapple.
  • Break a pin on self or other. You must win an opposed grapple check against the grappler pinning. He gets +2 to this check for every ally he has in the grapple.
  • Attack with another's light weapon. You must win an opposed grapple check against them. If you succeed, you can make a normal attack against them without penalty with that weapon.
  • Other: the DM will need to rule on what other things are possible or impossible.

If you are pinned:

  • You can't do anything but attempt to escape the pin. This is the same as trying to escape the grapple, but you are at -4 and if you succeed you are still grappled, just not pinned.
  • You suffer a -2 penalty to AC in addition to the flat-footed penalty from grappling against those not in the grapple.
  • Other grappling consequences still apply.

If you are pinning:

  • You may make the normal grapple actions above, but they must be directed against who you are pinning. You get +4 on these checks.
  • You may also attempt to disarm them of well-secured items. They get +4 to resist this disarm.
  • You can release them from the pin as a free action.
Grab Attack
Standard Action

Grab is a special attack that monsters have, such as those with pincers.

It works in many ways like grapple. All opposed checks are calculated the same, except that the monster is treated as one size smaller for this purpose.

If you are grabbed:

  • You don't threaten any spaces with attacks of opportunity.
  • You are considered flat-footed against anyone but the monster.
  • You can't move.
  • If the monster moves, you move with him.
  • You can't attack with weapons larger than light (and even so at a -4 penalty).
  • You can't cast most spells (exception: instant teleports)
  • You can't attack anyone but the monster.
  • You can't draw weapons or items without winning an opposed grapple check.
  • You can't dual-wield.
  • You can make a grapple or Escape check to escape.
  • Allies can make a grapple check against the monster to free you. (They don't need to join a grapple or anything.)
  • If you are held in the monster's mouth, you automatically fail saving throws against the monster's breath weapon.

The monster:

  • Can't use the limb or appendage except against you.
  • Can fling you away as an attack. You are flung 10' per size category of the monster above medium (10 for large, 20 for huge, etc.), minimum 10'. You suffer 1d6 damage for every 10' you are flung (doubled if you hit a rigid object like a wall).
  • Can fling you at a creature as an attack. This works the same as above, except the monster needs to make an attack roll against the target. If they hit, both you and the target take the same amount of damage. If they miss, you still take the flinging damage.
  • Can deal damage with the grabbing limb or appendage by winning an opposed grapple check. Some monsters deal bonus damage by doing this.
  • Can use the held creature as a shield (+2 shield bonus to AC). This requires a move action.
  • Otherwise suffers no consequences.

Bull-rush Attack
Standard Action

Bull-rush an opponent by moving into an opponent's space.

You and the opponent make opposed attack rolls.

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger and creatures smaller than medium get a -4 penalty for each category smaller.

If you win the opposed check, you can move the opponent back 5 feet + 5 feet for every 5 points you beat the defender by (your choice as to exactly how far), but you must spend movement as normal and move with your opponent to drive them back.

Disarm Attack
Standard Action

Make opposed attack rolls with the target. Users of two-handed weapons get +4 to this roll.

The defender receives a +4 bonus to this roll.

You can make an unarmed disarm attempt, but you suffer -4 on this roll.

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger, and creatures smaller than medium get a -4 penalty for each category smaller.

If the attacker wins this roll, the weapon is now out of the defender's hands and is on the ground in his space or in a space next to him (attacker's choice which space).

If the attacker was attempting an unarmed disarm attempt the weapon may be in his hand instead of on the ground.

Loosely secured items can be grabbed off a target by following the same procedure above.

Well-secured items (like a helmet or gauntlet) can only be disarmed if you have pinned the other character and the defender gets a +4 bonus.

Lock Weapons
Standard Action

Make a disarm attack, but at +4. If you succeed, you have locked weapons with your opponent.

Neither of you can move from the other.

Neither of you can use that weapon.

The instigator can end this condition as a free action at any time.

The victim can end the condition by succeeding on an opposed disarm check or by dropping their weapon.

If the victim drops their weapon, the instigator can cast it 10' away as a reaction.

This condition also ends when the victim is no longer within the instigator's reach (by forced movement).

Sunder Weapon
Standard Action

Make opposed attack rolls with the target.

Users of two-handed weapons get +4 to this roll.

The weapon must be capable of sundering things (no sundering a longsword with a spear).

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger, and creatures smaller than medium get a -4 penalty for each category smaller.

If the attacker wins this roll, he may roll damage to the weapon.

For each HP increment's worth of damage he deals to the weapon, one notch of durability is subtracted from the weapon.

A weapon with no notches of durability is destroyed. See the weapon for specific HP increment.

Sunder Armor/Shield Attack
Standard Action

Make an attack roll against AC 5 + armor bonus.

If you hit, roll damage to the armor.

For each HP increment's worth of damage you deal to the armor, one notch of durability is subtracted from the armor.

Each notch of durability lost is a -1 penalty to that armor's armor bonus to AC.

Armor that grants no armor bonus to AC is destroyed.

See the armor for specific HP increment.

The above rules equally apply to Shields, substituting references to armor or armor bonuses to AC with shield and shield bonuses to AC.

Movement

You can spend movement before or after actions, given that something has granted you movement this turn such as the walk action. For example, you can take the walk action (to be able to move your speed this round), move 10 feet, make a standard action attack against a goblin, kill it, and then move 20 more feet.

You can attempt to move through other creatures by making an Overrun attempt.

You may overrun during the course of a charge.

Overrun
Special

You may make a single overrun attempt during your turn.

You can only overrun creatures at most one size category larger than you.

If you allow them to move aside and they choose to move aside, then you move through their space with no consequence (not even an attack of opportunity from the movement) and this does not count as your overrun attempt.

Otherwise, you and your opponent make opposed attack rolls, adding your BAB.

A creature larger than medium gets +4 for every size category larger and creatures smaller than medium get a -4 penalty for each category smaller.

If you win the opposed check, then you knock your opponent prone and keep moving through them, without provoking attacks of opportunity.

You must move at least 10' straight through them and are not allowed to double back.

If you or your mount deal trample damage, the damage is automatically inflicted if you win.

Otherwise, you are knocked prone instead and your movement immediately ends.

Some creatures (like horses) are not knocked prone on a failed attempt and can attempt multiple overruns in a round. If they fail, their movement instead ceases.

Dying

Characters die at -10 HP or when circumstances dictate they should die.

A character at negative HP must roll to stabilize at a 10% chance at the beginning of their turn.

Characters who are not stable loses 1 HP at the beginning of their turn.

A character who is healed or granted temporary hit points stabilizes.

A character can be stabilized by a DC 15 wisdom check as a full-round action.

Circumstantial Rules

No Merry Go Rounds

Firing into a crowd

Ranged attackers are obligated to target randomly between potential enemy targets who are near one another

You can ignore this penalty by suffering a -4 penalty to your attack roll(s).

Cover

A target behind cover receives a bonus to AC from attacks on the other side of cover, dependent on the extend of the cover.

A creature can use a move action to "take cover". This doubles the benefit of the cover until they leave it, either because they move or they take an action that requires leaving the cover.

A creature behind total cover cannot be directly targeted or attacked.

Cover Bonus Example
Partial +2 Standing behind a waist-high table or wall. Hiding around a corner of a wall
Greater +4 Standing behind a crenellation or chest high wall
Near Total +8 Standing behind an arrow-slit

High Ground

Attacks from the high ground receive a +1 bonus.

The difference in height needs to be at least 2 feet for melee attacks and 6 feet for ranged attacks.

Flat-footed

A character that is flat-footed is caught unawares. Their AC is 2 points lower against attacks.

Concealment

Some effects (such as fog, blindness, invisbility or darkness) grant some degree of concealment.

Concealment is rated at either 20% or 50%.

Whenever an attack is made against a target with concealment, first roll the concealment percentage to see if it misses due to concealment.

If it does not, then roll the attack roll as normal.

In some circumstances, the DM may rule your attack will still need to be rolled, just not against who you intended. For example, firing at a figure you can barely see when he has a crowd behind him.

Mounted Combat

In battle, mounts move faster than when out of combat. A warhorse has a speed of 60 feet when in combat.

If you are riding a mount, you use your actions to move the mount, but use the mount's speed. Your mount is not allowed to take actions while ridden.

For example, if you want to charge while riding a destrier, you take a full-round action. The destrier moves 120 feet, at the end of which you can make a reckless attack, etc.

Mounts can overrun as part of their movement. They use your attack bonus, but their size. A mount's description will list if this inflicts any trample damage.

While you are mounted, you can only use 1-handed weapons, not light weapons or two-handed weapons. While you are mounted and your opponent is not, you are considered to have the high ground.

A confused character does not make attacks of opportunity against any creature that it is not already devoted to attacking (either because of its most recent action or because it has just been attacked).
