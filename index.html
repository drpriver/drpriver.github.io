How to Make a Character

  • Roll your attributes
  • Pick a class
  • Choose equipment
  • Name your character
  • Roll height and age
  • Determine your languages
  • Decide on other personal characteristics (personality, eye color, etc.)

You can choose to do these in any order you like, but you probably want to roll your attributes first.

Players may make as many characters as they like. The referee may limit the number of player-characters playable at once.

Each character starts play with 200gp, which they can use to purchase their starting equipment

If a character dies, the items they brought to play from character creation is assumed to be destroyed.

Attributes

Attributes are rated from 0—10. To determine your starting attribute, roll 2d6−2 for each one.

Attributes are mostly meant to determine what your character is like and for adjudicating certain situations (roll dexterity to pick locks for example).

Attribute Description
Strength Examples: Smash door, climb tree
Agility Examples: Balance, sneak
Dexterity Examples: Picking a lock or pocket
Intelligence Examples: Decipher script, identify magic item
Charisma Examples: Diplomacy, Bluffing
Wisdom Examples: Binding a wound

Attributes do not change your attacks, saving throws, AC, etc.

Leveling Up

A character who has earned sufficient XP may level up by spending some amount of downtime and resources to train.

A character can only level up during downtime periods.

Gaining multiple levels is permitted.

How to Level Up

Leveling up is mostly self-explanatory: Update your values according to your class's table and make any choices offered to you.

Hit points are rolled using the Sticky-Up method.

Sticky-Up HP Rolling

When you gain a new level, roll the dice indicated in your class's HP column. If you rolled equal to or lower than your previous HP total, your HP increases by 1. Otherwise, your HP becomes the number you rolled.

Thus, your HP is rerolled at each level, but can only go up (sticky-up).

Adventuring

Marching Order

When travelling through potentially dangerous territory, such as the wilderness or dungeon, the players must present a marching order for the DM. Unless otherwise stated or obviously implied by the situation, the characters will be in their marching order.

Attribute Checks

If you need to make an attribute check, roll a d20 + your attribute score against a DC set by the referee.

Double Jeopardy

In almost all cases, the same action cannot be attempted twice. For example, if a character fails to pick a lock, the party cannot retry that lock. They will need to do something substantively different, usually escalating the situation.

Hit Points

Characters and creatures have hit points (HP), representing how much damage they can take before they are in trouble. Whenever a character or creature suffers damage, that many hit points is subtracted from their current HP total.

Maximum Hit Points

Characters and creatures have a maximum number of hit points that they can have at any one time. This number is typically increased by gaining levels.

If an effect such as healing would increase a creature's current hit point total above their maximum hit points, their current hit points is instead set to their maximum hit points.

Temporary Hit Points

Hit points are normally permanent and are only lost through damage. In contrast, temporary hit points are just that - temporary. They only last for a specified duration, or until the end of the adventure session, whichever comes first.

These temporary hit points are lost first when the creature suffers damage and are not counted when determining a creature's current hit points. Thus temporary hit points are a way to have more hit points than your maximum hit points.

Temporary hit points do not stack. If you currently have temporary hit points and would gain more temporary hit points, you instead take the larger of the two.

Natural Healing

Characters heal (regain) their level in hit points each night. If they rest all day, they heal twice as much.

First Aid

Characters can be healed by use of a healer's kit. A character must have at least 5 wisdom to treat another character with a healer's kit. Using a healer's kit takes 1 minute, heals 1d6+3 hit points and consumes a usage of the kit.

Unnatural Healing

Some spells, magic items or other wondrous effects can heal characters. Such healing is only limited by whatever rules are associated with that spell, magic item, etc.

Dying

Characters die at −10 HP or when circumstances dictate they should die.

If in combat, measure time in rounds as usual. If not in combat, then begin measuring time in rounds to determine if you are able to save the character in time.

A character at negative HP must roll to stabilize at a 10% chance at the beginning of their turn.

Characters who are not stable loses 1 HP at the beginning of their turn.

A character who is healed or granted temporary hit points stabilizes.

A character can be stabilized by a DC 15 wisdom check as a full-round action. Only one character may make this check each round.

Damage

Ouch!

Damage Reduction

Sometimes referred to as DR, Damage Reduction reduces the amount of damage received from physical attacks, such as from weapons. It does not reduce the damage from fire, lightning, etc.

Damage Reduction is applied separately to each instance of damage.

If you have multiple sources of Damage Reduction, only the best one applies.

Resistance

Resistance to a type of damage means you take half damage from that kind of damage, rounded up.

Encumbrance

Slots are a measurement of how encumbering an item is. Each item will specify how many slots they are or will be determined by the referee.

A character can carry 10 + strength slots of items without being encumbered.

They receive a −1 penalty to attack and AC for each slot beyond that they are carrying.

Two day's worth of food is 1 slot.

Weapons are 1 slot, except for two-handed weapons which are 2 slots.

Jumping

To determine how far you can jump horizontally, roll 2d10 + your strength.

To determine how far you can jump vertically, roll d4 + half your strength.

In practice, the target number is typically set by what you are trying to jump across or to and you need to roll that number.

You can jump a horizontal distance up to your speed and a vertical distance up to 1.5x your height, rounded up to the nearest 5' increment.

For every size category smaller than medium, you get −4 to your jump checks. For every size category larger than medium, add +4 to your jump checks.

For every 5' slower than 30', you get −2 to jump. For every 5' faster than 30', you get +2 to jump.

Jumping in combat is normally a part of your movement for the turn.

If something allows to jump as a specific action, then the amount of distance you jump is extra movement granted by that action.

In combat, you cannot jump farther than your remaining movement for the round.

Falling

Characters suffer 1d6 damage for every 10' fallen.

Survival

Food and Water

After 3 days, a character must make a fortitude save (DC 10 + 1 for each day without food beyond the 3 days) each day or suffer 1d6 unhealable damage.

Each day of food heals 1d6 of this damage.

After 1 day without water, a character must make a fortitude save (DC 10 + 5 for each day without water) each day or suffer 3d6 unhealable damage.

Each day of water heals 1d6 of this damage.

If characters go on half-rations, they suffer −2 to all d20 rolls but eat half as many rations each day and stave off the starvation damage.

Heat and Humidity

A character in excessive clothing or wearing armor suffers 1d6 unhealable heat exhaustion damage each hour in hot and humid climates.

An hour in cool shade with water heals 1d6 of this damage.

Cold and Freezing

A characer in inadequate clothing suffers 1d6 unhealable freezing damage each hour in cold or arctic climates.

An hour in shelter with fire heals 1d6 of this damage.

In even extremer cold, the rate of damage is increased.

Suffocation

A character without air suffers 1d6 suffocation damage each minute without air, beginning at 2 minutes.

Mapping

The players may map the dungeon if they so desire. The map is both an in-game and out of game object. If it is lost in the game, the DM will take it away. If it is lost out of game, the characters no longer have access to it.

If a player character wishes to map, they must use both hands and have paper and ink and quill.

Light

Torches shed bright light in a 20' radius and shadowy light in a 20' radius beyond that. Torches burn for 1 hour.

Lanterns shed bright light in a 40' radius and shadowy light in a 40' radius beyond that. Lanterns burn at the rate of 1oz per hour.

The numbers above assume they are held in the hand or a similar elevation. If they are placed on the ground, they illuminate half as much. Dropping them unexpectedly risks them going out (50% chance).

Saving Throw

Sometimes referred to as a save, a saving throw is a roll to avoid or mitigate some harm.

There are 3 kinds of saving throws, Fortitude, Reflex and Will, which represent your ability to avoid harm due to your toughness, your reflexes or your willpower.

Every character has a BSB (Base Save Bonus) which they will add to any saving throw they make. Additionally, some classes and effects grant bonuses or penalties to specific kinds of saving throws.

A saving throw is made by rolling a d20 + bonuses against a DC.

Magic Item Division

Magic items and other useful items that are not easily divisible should be given out according to the following method:

  1. The magic items are sorted in order of most desirable to least desirable.
  2. The most desirable item remaining is up for claim.
  3. Any character who could reasonably use that item may make a claim. Each player can only make a single claim, no matter the number of characters they control.
  4. Amongst those who made a claim for an item, each rolls a d20 and whoever rolls the highest number is the owner of the item. In the event of a tie, there should be a roll-off between the highest rollers.
  5. Repeat this process until each item is claimed. It is courtesy to not make a claim for items once you have won one, unless no one else wants it.

Crusader Maneuvers

Due to your weak tie to Venrik, you occasionally are seized with divine inspiration. When not stressed due to battle or other extenuating circumstances, choose 5 of the maneuvers you know. Those maneuvers are prepared for you.

Management of maneuvers is simulated by use of playing cards. Each card represents one of the maneuvers you have prepared. At the beginning of your first turn of the encounter draw three (3) cards at random from the 5 maneuvers you've prepared. At the beginning of your following turns, draw an additional card from the maneuvers you haven't drawn yet. When you use a maneuver, place the card in your discard pile. If you have no more maneuvers you can draw, discard your current hand of cards and shuffle it into your discard pile. That pile then becomes the new pool you can draw from. Immediately draw two cards.

At higher levels you will know more maneuvers than you can prepare. To change your prepared maneuvers, you can spend 10 minutes meditating and praying to Venrik.

When you gain a level in this class, you can choose a maneuver you know and replace it with a different maneuver of the same level.

There are 3 kinds of maneuvers: Strikes, Counters and Boosts.

Unless otherwise specified, strikes are a standard action to use and involve you making a melee standard attack. Counters are always a reaction action. Unless otherwise specified, boosts are swift actions.

Enlightened Focus

As a disiciple of the enlightened, you have gained the ability to bring order to the chaotic elements of your soul. By meditating for 1 minute, you can remove the chaos from your system.

This state of balance lasts until it is disrupted. Roll a d6 at the beginning of each turn in a stressful situation like combat. If you roll a 4, 5, or 6, your balance is disrupted. By disrupting it, you achieve a temporary state of enlightenment, walking in the footsteps of those that came before you — Gentle Breeze and Kruglauk.

While in this state of enlightenment, you gain the following benefits:

  • You partially ignore the tragedy of flesh — You have resistance to all kinds of damage.
  • Your mind is shielded from the influence of others — You automatically succeed on any saving throws against fear, rage, or other mental effects such as mind control.
  • Your blows strike swift and true — You can make an unarmed melee standard attack as a free action each round.

These benefits last until you are unconscious, the battle is over or 10 minutes pass, whichever comes soonest.

Unarmed and Unarmored

While wearing no armor, your base AC is higher than 10. The value is given in your class table. You are deftly able to dodge blows due to your ability to perceive reality in a different way.

Additionally, while wearing no armor your speed is increased by 10.

You are deadly with the fist. Your unarmed strikes deal extra damage and can be dual-wielded.

You do not receive a penalty when attempting an unarmed disarm attempt.

You do not need to be unarmed to make unarmed attacks. For example, you can kick or headbutt, even if your hands are full.

Dragon Knight Maneuvers

The Dragon Knights of Bahamut train vigorously and are rewarded with special powers. Outside of battle, choose to ready a number of maneuvers as given by your class table. You cannot ready the same maneuver more than once. When a battle starts, you have immediate access to the maneuvers you have readied. Once you have used one of the maneuvers you have readied, you cannot use it again until you are recentered.

You can recenter by taking a refocus action. This action grants you a melee standard attack.

At higher levels you will know more maneuvers than you can prepare. To change your prepared maneuvers, you can spend 10 minutes meditating.

When you gain a level in this class, you can choose a maneuver you know and replace it with a different maneuver of the same level.

There are 3 kinds of maneuvers: Strikes, Counters and Boosts.

Unless otherwise specified, strikes are a standard action to use and involve you making a melee standard attack. Counters are always a reaction action. Unless otherwise specified, boosts are swift actions.

Animal Forms

Shapechangers have the unique ability to transform into an animal that they have established a spirit bond with. The number of bonds they can establish is limited by their level, as given in the class table above.

To establish a spirit bond with an animal, the shapechanger must seek out and befriend the animal and either make a deal with it or earn its trust. The shapechanger and the animal then link their spirits together and the shapechanger is then able to turn into that specific animal.

If the shapechanger dies, all the animals it has bonded become heartbroken.

The shapechanger may change shape whenever they want and however many times they want. Changing shape is an instinctual action. In combat, it is a move action. The shapechanger can switch between animal forms or back to their normal form. The shapechanger remains in the animal form until they consciously change shape again.

Items worn or carried by the shapechanger do not change shape with them. For this reason, they often wear loose robes and avoid rings and jewelry.

While in animal form, the shapechanger enjoys all the benefits and drawbacks implicit to that animal form. For example, a shark can "breathe" water, but is nearly helpless on land. Other benefits such as attack forms, AC changes, etc. are listed in the below stat blocks for convenience.

If a stat block lists an HP bonus, your maximum and current HP are increased by that amount while you are in the form. If you leave the form, they both reduce by that amount, with the exception that you are not reduced below 1 current or maximum HP by this change. However, the damage is "remembered" -- when you shift back into an animal form you still have that amount of damage. This damage must be healed first by any healing effect before you can be raised above 1 HP.

If a stat block lists an attack form, it will list first an attack bonus and then the damage. The bonus is on top of your BAB and other bonuses.

Equipment

Proficiency

A character cannot use any item, wear any armor or attack with any weapon that they are not proficient with.

Proficiency with some items is implied rather than explicitly stated — a character knows how to tie their own shoes.

Proficiency will be explicitly stated for the use of weapons, armors, wands, scrolls and staves.

Durability

Items have 3 "notches" of durability.

When an item is out of notches, it is destroyed or broken.

A weapon gets −1 penalty to attack for each notch missing.

Armor gets a −1 penalty to AC for each notch missing. A damaged magic device has a 5% chance of mishap for each notch missing.

Notches are usually lost from sundering, but the DM may rule that certain effects like fireballs can damage weapons too if the user fails a save.

Range Increment

Ranged weapons and thrown weapons have a range increment.

For every full range-increment you suffer a –2 penalty to the attack. For example, if your weapon has a range increment of 15' and you attack from 35' away you suffer a –4.

Ranged weapons can attack up to 10 range increments away

Thrown Weapons can attack up to 5 range increments away

Wands

Wands have notches of durability, just as weapons do, also breaking and being destroyed when the 3rd notch of durability is gone.

Unlike weapons, wands lose a notch of durability when a natural 1 is rolled for their attack roll. If the wand does not have an attack rule (like the wand of healing), roll a d20 when you use it. On a 1, the wand loses a notch of durability. On a 20, the effect of the wand is doubled.

Additionally, wands may have special abilities that can be used. These special abilities may have a higher change of damaging a notch of durability or automatically losing a notch of durability.

Wands cannot be repaired by any means.

If a player wants to hire an NPC to work for them or to perform a specific task, see these rules.

Loyalty

Sometimes the loyalty of a hireling or companion will be tested, such as when facing a fire-breathing dragon or when faced with a large treasure. A loyalty test is rolled by rolling 2d6 + the character's current loyalty score.

A roll of 7 or higher indicates that the character remains loyal, while a result below that indicates betrayal or shirking of duty.

A failed loyalty test can manifest in different ways based on circumstance:

  • A failed loyalty test in combat can indicate fleeing or abandonment.
  • A failed loyalty test when faced with treasure indicates stealing it.
  • Etc.

Companion descriptions list example circumstances that dictate need to test loyalty, but the referee can decide that certain circumstances dictate a check not listed.

The loyalty of a companion or hireling starts at 0. This number increases due to positive interactions and decreases due to negative ones. In general, every doubling of pay increases starting loyalty of a hireling by 1. Death of a non-combatant hireling decreases the base loyalty of all hirelings by 2 (stacking) until the memory fades.

Positive or negative reputation also changes the base loyalty of new hirelings and companions.

When multiple hirelings of a single type are hired (such as mercenaries), the referee may assume all of their loyalty scores are the same.

Combat Rules

Initiative and the Flow of Combat

At the beginning of combat, each side rolls a d20. The side of the high roller gets to go first. Each turn after that alternates between the two sides.

All characters on a side can take their turns and actions at the same time on their side's turn, or process them one at a time.

The beginning or end of a turn for a character is synonymous with the beginning or end of a turn for that character's side.

If the d20 roll is a tie, it is a simultaneous turn. First one of the players goes, then one of the monsters goes, then one of the players goes, etc. The players can choose who they want to go, but they must choose within 10 seconds or lose their chance to go.

Surprise

If one side has achieved surprise over the other, they get a free round before initiative is rolled. During this round, the members of their side can only take a single standard action each. The side that has been surprised is considered flat-footed.

Actions in Combat

During each round, you may take a move-action, standard-action, swift-action and reaction.

A standard-action and a move-action may be combined together to take a full-action.

A standard-action can be used to take a move-action.

A standard-action can be readied to be performed contingent on some other visible action or effect. Readying an action this way does not require your reaction. The trigger must be perceivable by the character from an in-game perspective. For example, you can ready an action for when someone attacks any ally or draws their weapon, but not contingent on the start of someone's turn.

Swift-actions must happen during your turn. Any action that requires a swift-action will specify when it may be used. No other action type may be substituted for a swift-action.

Reactions can be taken during your turn or during anothers turn. Once you have taken a reaction you cannot take one until the beginning of your next turn.

A free action does not require the use of any action (it is free). However, some free actions may be limited in how many times they can be repeated.

Attack Roll

When you attack a creature, you must roll an attack roll to determine whether or not you harm them. An attack roll is a d20 + your attack bonus. If you equal or exceed their AC with this roll, you have hit them and can roll damage.

Calculating Attack Bonus

Normally, your attack bonus is your Base Attack Bonus (BAB) from your class, plus any circumstantial modifiers. Some items, class features or other effects might also grant you a bonus to your attacks.

Critical Hit

If you roll a natural 20 (or another natural number in your weapon's critical hit range), you have threatened a critical hit. Immediately make another attack roll with the same bonus. If that attack roll hits, then you have confirmed the critical hit. By default, weapons have a x2 critical hit multiplier. This means you roll your damage and add your bonuses twice. For example, if you deal 1d6+3 damage on a normal hit, a critical hit will deal 1d6+3 + 1d6+3 = 2d6+6 damage.

Some weapons have x3 or even x4 critical hit multipliers. In those cases, roll your damage and add your bonuses that many times instead.

Spells with attack rolls can critically hit. They threaten on a natural 20 and have a x2 multiplier. If the spell does not deal damage, then critical hits have no extra effect.

Some weapons specify a special effect upon a critical hit. Look to those weapons for the description.

Attack of Opportunity

An attack of opportunity is a reaction that is performed in response to some activity that lowers the opponent's guard.

Some actions specify they provoke an attack of opportunity.

Additionally, a character moving away from you or moving within your reach provokes an attack of opportunity from you.

If a character has reach and you attempt to move beyond the edge of their reach towards them, you also provoke an attack of opportunity, even if you are moving into their deadzone.

Ranged Attacks provoke an attack of opportunity.

Most spells provoke attacks of opportunity. Melee attack spells, touch spells cast on allies, or spells originating from the caster with a length or radius of 15 ft or less do not provoke attacks of opportunity.

Drinking a potion or getting something out of your pack provokes an attack of opportunity.

Standing up from prone provokes an attack of opportunity.

If a character provokes an attack of opportunity from you, you may make an immediate, melee standard attack against them. You must be wielding a melee weapon in order to make this attack. If your unarmed strikes deal lethal damage, you are considered to be wielding a melee weapon for this purpose.

If you can somehow make more than one attack of opportunity each round, you can only make one for each opportunity. In this case, movement through your threatened spaces is only considered a single opportunity.

If a creature provokes an attack of opportunity from more than one creature, only two creatures may actually make an attack of opportunity.

Movement

You can spend movement before or after actions, given that something has granted you movement this turn such as the walk action. For example, you can take the walk action (to be able to move your speed this round), move 10 feet, make a standard action attack against a goblin, kill it, and then move 20 more feet.

You can attempt to move through other creatures by making an overrun attempt.

You may overrun during the course of a charge.

No Merry Go Rounds

Firing into a crowd

Ranged attackers are obligated to target randomly between potential enemy targets who are near one another

You can ignore this penalty by suffering a −4 penalty to your attack roll(s).

Cover

A target behind cover receives a bonus to AC from attacks on the other side of cover, dependent on the extend of the cover.

A creature can use a move action to "take cover". This doubles the benefit of the cover until they leave it, either because they move or they take an action that requires leaving the cover.

A creature behind total cover cannot be directly targeted or attacked.

Reach

Reach is how far away you can attack. By default, weapons and creatures have 5' of reach, thus they can only attack creatures adjacent to them.

Some weapons, such as the spear, have 10' of reach. This means they can attack creatures up to 10' away, or within 2 spaces. However, the spear also has a 5' deadzone, meaning it can't attack creatures 5' away or closer. Thus a creature with a spear can only attack someone 5-10' away.

High Ground

Attacks from the high ground receive a +1 bonus.

The difference in height needs to be at least 2 feet for melee attacks and 6 feet for ranged attacks.

Concealment

Some effects (such as fog, blindness, invisbility or darkness) grant some degree of concealment.

Concealment is rated at either 20% or 50%.

Whenever an attack is made against a target with concealment, first roll the concealment percentage to see if it misses due to concealment.

If it does not, then roll the attack roll as normal.

In some circumstances, the DM may rule your attack will still need to be rolled, just not against who you intended. For example, firing at a figure you can barely see when he has a crowd behind him.

Mounted Combat

In battle, mounts move faster than when out of combat. A warhorse has a speed of 60 feet when in combat.

If you are riding a mount, you use your actions to move the mount, but use the mount's speed. Your mount is not allowed to take actions while ridden.

For example, if you want to charge while riding a destrier, you take a full-round action. The destrier moves 120 feet, at the end of which you can make a reckless attack, etc.

Mounts can overrun as part of their movement. They use your attack bonus, but their size. A mount's description will list if this inflicts any trample damage.

While you are mounted, you can only use 1-handed weapons, not light weapons or two-handed weapons.

While you are mounted and your opponent is not, you are considered to have the high ground.

Combat Actions

This chapter covers the rules for various actions you can take in combat. You can take other actions in combat, which will be resolved in the spirit of the following rules. The actions below cover the most common ones.

Movement Actions

Walk

Move Action
This round you can move your speed.

Hustle

Full-round Action
This round you can move double your speed.

Run

Full-round Action
This round you can move 4x your speed. You are considered flat-footed against any attack.

Stand Up

Move Action
Standing up from prone provkes an attack of opportunity.

Withdraw

Full-round Action
This round you can move double your speed. Creatures who started your turn threatening you cannot take attacks of opportunity against you.

Reposition

Free Action*
You can only take this action if you haven't otherwise moved. After taking this action, you also cannot move. You can move 5 feet without provoking attacks of opportunity as long as you remain within the reach of everyone who threatens attacks of opportunity against you.

Overrun

Special

You may make a single overrun attempt during your turn.

You can only overrun creatures at most one size category larger than you.

If you allow them to move aside and they choose to move aside, then you move through their space with no consequence (not even an attack of opportunity from the movement) and this does not count as your overrun attempt.

Otherwise, you and your opponent make opposed attack rolls.

A creature larger than medium gets +4 for every size category larger and creatures smaller than medium get a −4 penalty for each category smaller.

If you win the opposed check, then you knock your opponent prone and keep moving through them, without provoking attacks of opportunity.

You must move at least 10' straight through them and are not allowed to double back.

If you or your mount deal trample damage, the damage is automatically inflicted if you win.

Otherwise, you are knocked prone instead and your movement immediately ends.

Some creatures (like horses) are not knocked prone on a failed attempt and can attempt multiple overruns in a round. If they fail, their movement instead ceases.

Melee or Ranged Attacks

All ranged attacks provoke an attack of opportunity.

Standard Attack

Standard Action
  • Make an attack roll against an enemy.
  • If you meet or beat their AC, deal normal damage to them.

Dual Wield Attack

Standard Action

Make an attack with each weapon in your hand, but at a −2 penalty.

Each hit deals normal damage.

Each weapon must be a light weapon or the two heads of a double weapon.

Rapid Attack

Full-round Action

Make two attacks at −4 attack. Each Deals normal damage.

Called Shot

Standard Action

A called shot is a special attack made at a specific location.

Suffer a penalty based on which location, but inflict status effect if you hit in addition to rolling normal damage.

You must be able to see your target clearly (no concealment or blindness).

The DM may make specific rulings to allow other called shots or variant effects (such as attacking a tentacle in order to free a comrade from a grab).

The penalty from arms and hands do not stack with one another.

The penalty from leg and foot also do not stack with one another.

Choose one of the locations and roll appropriately:

Melee Only Attacks

Reckless Attack

Standard Action

Make a single attack at +2 attack, but suffer −2 AC until the beginning of your next turn.

You must be threatened with attacks of opportunity by your target to take this action.

Guarded Attack

Standard Action
Make a single attack at −2 attack, but gain +2 AC until the beginning of your next turn.

Charge Attack

Full-round Action

Move up to double your speed in a straight line, at the end of which make a Reckless Attack, Drive Back or Bull Rush Attack.

If you charge on a mount, you deal double damage with a lance if you hit.

You may make an overrun attempt during the movement portion of a charge.

Drive Back Attack

Standard Action

Your opponent either moves back 5 feet or stands still.

If they move back 5 feet, you can follow for free and make a single standard attack against them at −2.

If they choose to stand still, you can make a single standard attack against them at +2.

If they move back 5 feet, you may immediately take the walk action to grant yourself movement.

The opponent must have somewhere they can move back to for you to take this action.

Grapple

Standard Action

To start a grapple, you and the opponent make opposed grapple check.

A grapple check is rolled by making an unarmed attack roll.

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger and creatures smaller than medium get a −4 penalty for each category smaller.

If you win this check, you and your opponent are now grappling (both grappled) and you deal damage as if you hit with an unarmed strike.

You can't force a creature two sizes larger than you to grapple and such a creature can automatically escape such a grapple if he finds himself in one.

You and your opponent are both considered to be occupying both your space and their space.

Any ranged attacks into the grapple are equally likely to hit the other person instead on accident.

If the creatures are of different size, the ratio is adjusted accordingly.

Multiple creatures can be in a grapple.

Once a grapple is initiated, it is a standard action to join the grapple, but you do not need to beat any grapple check to join.

If you need to roll an opposed check while in the grapple, pick an enemy grappler at random to oppose you.

If you are grappled:

  • You don't threaten any spaces with attacks of opportunity.
  • You are considered flat-footed against attackers not in the grapple.
  • You can't move from the grapple (see below).
  • You can't attack with weapons larger than light weapons.
  • You can't attack outside of the grapple.
  • You can't cast most spells (exception: instant teleports)
  • You can't draw weapons (see below) or items.
  • You can't dual-wield.

Attacks while grappling:

  • Attack with a natural or light weapon at a −4 penalty.
  • Damage your opponent. Make an opposed grapple check. If you win, you deal unarmed strike damage.
  • Draw a light-weapon. This is a standard action and requires winning an opposed grapple check.
  • Move the grapple. You can move the grapple up to half your speed by winning an opposed grapple check.
  • Escape the grapple. You must win an opposed grapple check (you may use Escape) against the enemy grappler in the grapple. He gets +2 to this check for every ally he has in the grapple.
  • Pin an opponent. You must win an opposed grapple check (he may use Escape) against the enemy grappler in the grapple. You get +2 to this check for every ally you have in the grapple.
  • Break a pin on self or other. You must win an opposed grapple check against the grappler pinning. He gets +2 to this check for every ally he has in the grapple.
  • Attack with another's light weapon. You must win an opposed grapple check against them. If you succeed, you can make a normal attack against them without penalty with that weapon.
  • Other: the referee will need to rule on what other things are possible or impossible.

If you are pinned:

  • You can't do anything but attempt to escape the pin. This is the same as trying to escape the grapple, but you are at −4 and if you succeed you are still grappled, just not pinned.
  • You suffer a −2 penalty to AC in addition to the flat-footed penalty from grappling against those not in the grapple.
  • Other grappling consequences still apply.

If you are pinning:

  • You may make the normal grapple actions above, but they must be directed against who you are pinning. You get +4 on these checks.
  • You may also attempt to disarm them of well-secured items. They get +4 to resist this disarm.
  • You can release them from the pin as a free action.

Grab

Standard Action

Grab is a special attack that monsters have, such as those with pincers. A creature capable of a grab can only grab creatures one size smaller than it or smaller.

It works in many ways like grapple. All opposed checks are calculated the same, except that the monster is treated as one size smaller for the purpose of calculating bonuses.

If you are grabbed:

  • You don't threaten any spaces with attacks of opportunity.
  • You are considered flat-footed against anyone but the monster.
  • You can't move.
  • If the monster moves, you move with him.
  • You can't attack with weapons larger than light (and even so at a −4 penalty).
  • You can't cast most spells (exception: instant teleports)
  • You can't attack anyone but the monster.
  • You can't draw weapons or items without winning an opposed grapple check.
  • You can't dual-wield.
  • You can make a grapple or Escape check to escape.
  • Allies can make a grapple check against the monster to free you. (They don't need to join a grapple or anything.)
  • If you are held in the monster's mouth, you automatically fail saving throws against the monster's breath weapon.

The monster:

  • Can't use the limb or appendage except against you.
  • Can fling you away as an attack. You are flung 10' per size category of the monster above medium (10 for large, 20 for huge, etc.), minimum 10'. You suffer 1d6 damage for every 10' you are flung (doubled if you hit a rigid object like a wall).
  • Can fling you at a creature as an attack. This works the same as above, except the monster needs to make an attack roll against the target. If they hit, both you and the target take the same amount of damage. If they miss, you still take the flinging damage.
  • Can deal damage with the grabbing limb or appendage by winning an opposed grapple check. Some monsters deal bonus damage by doing this.
  • Can use the held creature as a shield (+2 shield bonus to AC). This requires a move action.
  • Otherwise suffers no consequences.

Mark

Standard Action

Choose an enemy you are adjacent to.

If that enemy attacks anyone other than you with their first attack on their turn, you may take an attack of opportunity against them.

They remain marked until you no are no longer adjacent to them.

Trip Attack

Standard Action

You and the opponent make opposed attack rolls.

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger and creatures smaller than medium get a −4 penalty for each category smaller.

Some weapons give a bonus to this attack roll.

If you win the opposed check, the defender is knocked prone in their space. Standing up from prone is a move action that provokes an attack of opportunity.

Bull-rush

Standard Action

Bull-rush an opponent by moving into an opponent's space.

You and the opponent make opposed attack rolls.

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger and creatures smaller than medium get a −4 penalty for each category smaller.

If you were charging, you may add +2 to this check.

If you win the opposed check, you can move the opponent back 5 feet + 5 feet for every 5 points you beat the defender by (your choice as to exactly how far), but you must spend movement as normal and move with your opponent to drive them back.

If you drive them into a wall or similar hard, sturdy surface, they suffer 1d6 damage for every 5' you would have pushed them.

You may take a move action to walk while resolving this action.

Any movement caused by this maneuver provokes attacks of opportunity as normal, except the two involved in the struggle don't provoke against each other.

You and the defender don't provoke from each other, but you and the defender may provoke attacks from bystanders.

Disarm

Standard Action

Make opposed attack rolls with the target. Users of two-handed weapons get +4 to this roll.

The defender receives a +4 bonus to this roll.

You can make an unarmed disarm attempt, but you suffer −4 on this roll.

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger, and creatures smaller than medium get a −4 penalty for each category smaller.

If the attacker wins this roll, the weapon is now out of the defender's hands and is on the ground in his space or in a space next to him (attacker's choice which space).

If the attacker was attempting an unarmed disarm attempt the weapon may be in his hand instead of on the ground.

Loosely secured items can be grabbed off a target by following the same procedure above.

Well-secured items (like a helmet or gauntlet) can only be disarmed if you have pinned the other character and the defender gets a +4 bonus.

Lock Weapons

Standard Action

Make a disarm attack, but at +4.

If you succeed, you have locked weapons with your opponent.

Neither of you can move from the other.

Neither of you can use that weapon.

The instigator can end this condition as a free action at any time.

The victim can end the condition by succeeding on an opposed disarm check or by dropping their weapon.

If the victim drops their weapon, the instigator can cast it 10' away as a reaction.

This condition also ends when the victim is no longer within the instigator's reach (by forced movement).

Sunder Weapon

Standard Action

Make opposed attack rolls with the target.

Users of two-handed weapons get +4 to this roll.

The weapon must be capable of sundering things (no sundering a longsword with a spear).

Creatures larger than medium get +4 for each category larger, and creatures smaller than medium get a −4 penalty for each category smaller.

If the attacker wins this roll, he may roll damage to the weapon.

For each HP increment's worth of damage he deals to the weapon, one notch of durability is subtracted from the weapon.

A weapon with no notches of durability is destroyed. See the weapon for specific HP increment.

Sunder Armor/Shield

Standard Action

Make an attack roll against AC 5 + armor bonus.

If you hit, roll damage to the armor.

For each HP increment's worth of damage you deal to the armor, one notch of durability is subtracted from the armor.

Each notch of durability lost is a −1 penalty to that armor's armor bonus to AC.

Armor that grants no armor bonus to AC is destroyed.

See the armor for specific HP increment.

The above rules equally apply to Shields, substituting references to armor or armor bonuses to AC with shield and shield bonuses to AC.

Ranged Only Attacks

All ranged attacks provoke an attack of opportunity.

Grenade-like Attack

Standard Action

Choose a space.

AC is 5 + 1 for every 5' from you it is (or 5+1 for every 10' for wands).

If you hit, grenade lands in that spot.

If you miss, roll 1d6−1d6 for left-right divergence and 1d6−1d6 for near-far divergence.

Read the number as number of 5' spaces.

Grenades must travel at least 15' from you.

Where you rolled is where the grenade lands for the purpose of any effect it has.

Standard Action
You receive a +4 bonus to AC and Reflex saves until the beginning of your next turn.

Draw an Item

Move Action*

Some items may be a full-round action to draw (such as items buried in your pack).

Drawing or stowing an item provokes an attack of opportunity.

You can draw two-weapons at once if your hands are empty and you are going to dual-wield them.

You cannot draw an item off the ground if you are engaged in melee with an enemy.

Stow an Item

Move Action*

You may do this as a free action once per round.

Drawing or stowing an item provokes an attack of opportunity.

Shield Shatter

Reaction
When you are struck by a damaging attack or spell, you can choose to sacrifice your shield instead of you taking the hit. If you do so, you gain 1 point of exhaustion. Your AC is unaltered until your turn (this is purely for convenience).

Action type varies

Conditions

Ablaze

A creature that is ablaze is on fire.

Unless otherwise specified, they suffer 1d6 fire damage at the beginning of their turn unless they succeed at a DC 15 reflex save to put the fire out.

Rolling on the ground grants a new save against the fire at +4.

Dousing the flame immediately puts the fire out.

Blind

A blinded creature suffers −4 AC.

Any attack they made is against 50% concealment.

They threaten no spaces.

Confused

A confused character’s actions are determined by rolling d% at the beginning of his turn (see table below).

A confused character who can’t carry out the indicated action does nothing but babble incoherently.

Attackers are not at any special advantage when attacking a confused character.

Any confused character who is attacked automatically attacks its attackers on its next turn, as long as it is still confused when its turn comes.

A confused character does not make attacks of opportunity against any creature that it is not already devoted to attacking (either because of its most recent action or because it has just been attacked).

d% Result
01‑10 attack or charge an enemy or threat with melee or ranged weapons
11‑20 act normally
21‑50 do nothing but babble incoherently
51‑70 flee away at top possible speed
71‑100 attack nearest creature

Dazed

A dazed creature can only take one standard action on its turn and cannot take reactions.

Dazzled

A dazzled creature makes attacks at 20% concealment.

Entangled

An entangled creature is gooed up or tangled up in something.

An entangled creature moves at half speed and has −2 AC.

If the entanglement would affect their attacks, they get −2 to their attacks. For example, a tanglefoot bag entangles the feet so it has no effect on attacks, but being tangled up in a net would.

Fatigued (N)

A fatigued creature gets −2 to attacks, saves, ability checks for each level of fatigue they have.

Flat-footed

A flat-footed creatures gets −2 AC.

Frightened (N)

A creature suffers −2 to attacks, saves and ability checks for each level of fear they have.

An NPC with frightened 2 or 3 must immediately check morale.

Paralyzed

All attacks against a paralyzed creature hits and are critical hits.

A paralyzed creature cannot take actions or reactions.

Prone

A character that is prone gets −4 to AC against melee attacks and may have cover against ranged attacks.

It is a move action to stand up.

A character that is prone cannot make attacks.

Stunned

A stunned creature cannot take actions or reactions.

Surprised

A surprised creature is flat-footed and can't take reactions.

Unconscious

Any melee attack against an unconscious creature automatically hits and triggers a save or die.

Weakened

−2 to melee attacks

−2 to melee damage

−4 to strength checks
